# School Messaging Research
*Research for ISJ Website v4 — Competitive Messaging Analysis*
*Compiled: March 2026*

---

## Overview

This document analyses how five benchmark schools describe themselves, their teachers, and their educational philosophy. The aim is to identify the language, tone, and framing conventions used by the best British independent and prep schools — and to assess where ISJ's current messaging sits relative to that standard.

---

## 1. Dragon School Oxford
**dragonschool.org**

### Core Identity Statement

The Dragon positions itself around a single, recurring concept: **free thinking**. This is not a background value — it is the school's primary brand claim, used as a noun, an adjective, and an implicit character type.

> *"An extraordinary independent co-ed day and boarding Prep school in Oxford for children aged 4–13. Discover what it means to be a free thinking Dragon."*

> *"At the Dragon, we foster free thinkers and lifelong learners; we encourage our pupils to raise their hand, roll up their sleeves, join the dots and explore their passions. To reach for the sun, as we say here. Get ready to live curiously."*

The phrase **"reach for the sun"** functions as the school's unofficial motto — it appears repeatedly across the site, curriculum documents, and alumni communications.

### How They Describe Teachers

Dragon does not emphasise teacher qualifications or credentials in its public-facing messaging. Teachers are positioned as **enablers of curiosity**, not as credentialled authorities. The Deputy Head Academic is quoted directly on the website:

> *"Our curriculum is designed to inspire, motivate and challenge, ensuring that every child has the opportunity to succeed in their education. We value the journey over the destination and recognise that the best learning takes place in a stimulating and secure learning environment, where curiosity and enquiry are at the heart of the process."*
> — William Dickson, Deputy Head Academic

From Year 5 upwards, teaching is described as being led by **"passionate subject specialists"** — the word "passionate" does the heavy lifting; qualifications are not mentioned.

The school's foundational philosophy is captured in a quote from its founding head, still used on curriculum documents today:

> *"We have failed, unless we have helped the child develop his mind and his capacities in his own way, unless we have given him full scope for all the imagination and originality that is in him."*
> — Skipper Lynam, Head of the Dragon 1886–1920

### How They Frame Risk-Taking and Mistakes

This is a distinctive strength of Dragon's messaging. Mistakes are framed as **structurally valuable** — not merely tolerated, but celebrated as part of the learning process. From the ISI inspection report (2023):

> *"The school promotes and encourages a learning environment in which the pupils see the value of mistakes as learning opportunities, with teachers and leadership committed to this approach."*

> *"Pre-prep pupils were seen climbing trees to a height they were comfortable with, going on the big hammock and baking bread on the open fire. They felt safe in the knowledge they could make mistakes and be supported by the teachers in trying again."*

Alumni are quoted using the same language, reinforcing that this ethos is genuinely lived, not just stated:

> *"The Dragon was where I first learned how to think, not what to think... think big, stay curious, and don't be afraid to break the mold."*
> — Ted Christie-Miller (OD 2008), Founder of Residual

### How They Frame Academic Achievement

Dragon leads with **character and curiosity**, not exam results. Academic outcomes are presented as a natural consequence of the culture, not its primary driver. The curriculum aims state:

> *"Dragons are academically rigorous, creative, independent and ambitious free-thinkers. We inspire and encourage our pupils to reach for the sun in their learning and strive for excellent academic outcomes and experiences through a rich and progressive curriculum."*

The **DRACO** learning framework (Diligence, Resilience, Ambition, Collaboration, Organisation) provides a structure that ties academic habit to character — framing scholastic effort as a dimension of who you are, not just what you do.

The **Teaching Trilogy** — Culture, Curiosity, Challenge — is used as a consistent pedagogical framework across all classrooms and age groups.

Dragon Quest (a Saturday enrichment programme) is described as placing "equal weight on intellectual, emotional, social, and physical development."

### Overall Tone

- **Personal, energetic, and proud** — unabashedly confident that there is something distinctive and special about being a Dragon.
- Speaks directly to the child as much as the parent: *"Get ready to live curiously."*
- Avoids institutional language entirely; no passive voice, no dry descriptions of "facilities" or "curriculum frameworks."
- Uses **"we"** as a school community rather than a corporate entity.
- Embraces identity language: not "pupils" but **"Dragons"** throughout.

### Standout Phrases

- *"Reach for the sun"* — repeated leitmotif
- *"Get ready to live curiously"*
- *"Free thinkers, lifelong learners"*
- *"Kind and confident. Courageous and empathetic. Respectful and spirited."*
- *"It's hard to put your finger on what makes a Dragon, but you'll know it when you see it."*
- *"Above all, it's about being curious: about learning; about each other; about being a human in the world today."*
- *"We value the journey over the destination"*
- *"Think big, stay curious, and don't be afraid to break the mold"*
- *"Curious: about learning; about each other; about being a human in the world today"*

## 2. Summer Fields Oxford
**summerfields.com**

### Core Identity Statement

Summer Fields positions itself on **academic pedigree, pastoral intensity, and community**. Its tone is more traditional and less emotive than Dragon's, but it is warm and deeply family-oriented — much of its messaging stems from its near-total boarding model (most staff live on campus).

The school motto — **Mens Sana in Corpore Sano** ("A healthy mind in a healthy body") — anchors the messaging and is explicitly cited throughout.

> *"Summer Fields is one of the country's pre-eminent prep schools and the mission of both Governors and the Headmaster is to develop that position. Its character and reputation are based firstly on the quality of Summer Fields' academic teaching: boys are expected to stretch themselves and aspire to academic excellence."*
> — Mission Statement (Headmaster David Faber)

> *"We pride ourselves on the quality of our staff. They are drawn from many backgrounds, but share one thing — a passion to teach the boys of tomorrow."*

### How They Describe Teachers

Summer Fields offers one of the most explicit and memorable teacher descriptions among the schools surveyed:

> *"They are drawn from many backgrounds, but share one thing — a passion to teach the boys of tomorrow."*

This is deliberately non-credential-focused. It foregrounds **passion and shared purpose** over qualifications. The fact that 80% of staff live on campus is a key differentiator and is mentioned prominently — teachers are not just classroom professionals but **members of a live-in community**:

> *"Unlike many schools, most of the staff live on the campus and this enables them to play a very important part in the life of the school."*

The ISI Inspection Report (2025) noted: *"This individualised pastoral support has a highly positive impact on pupils' academic achievement and personal development and is a significant strength of the school."*

Teachers are also described as playing roles as Lodgeparents and tutors — their identity extends far beyond their subject.

### How They Frame Risk-Taking and Mistakes

Summer Fields does not explicitly use risk-taking language. The focus is more on **confidence, self-expression, and individuality**:

> *"Instil in the boys the confidence to discover and express their own individuality."*

> *"A Summerfieldian is confident in himself and proud of his achievements and has a real 'can do' attitude. He is confident but not arrogant and is keen to make the most of everything on offer at the school."*

> *"Confidence gained outside the classroom will encourage boys to do well in the classroom."*

### How They Frame Academic Achievement

Academic achievement is front-loaded but always contextualised within character development. The school leads with scholarships to leading senior schools as proof of academic quality, but immediately follows with the broader picture:

> *"A string of scholarships to leading public schools is testament to the success of this ambition."*

> *"To educate boys in the widest sense whereby the foundations for future success are laid at a young age."*

> *"Education is not confined to the classroom."*

> *"Our mission is to continue to provide Summerfieldians with the best chance to find and develop those talents latent in each and every one of them."*

The framing is outcomes-oriented but avoids being purely exam-focused. The school's stated aims explicitly call out **character and personality** as the real goals:

> *"To emphasise developing character and personality so that Summerfieldians can go out into the world and both achieve and contribute positively."*

### Overall Tone

- **Traditional, warm, and community-focused** — reads like a very good public school in its values and language.
- More formal than Dragon but not stiff; the boarding community gives it an organic warmth.
- Strong emphasis on **Christian values** and **manners**: *"Much importance is placed on traditional values including politeness, good manners, and sportsmanship."*
- Uses a distinctive pupil identity: **"Summerfieldian"** — used as a character type in the same way Dragon uses "Dragon."
- Academic confidence is quiet and assured, not boastful.

### Standout Phrases

- *"They are drawn from many backgrounds, but share one thing — a passion to teach the boys of tomorrow."*
- *"A healthy mind in a healthy body"* (Mens Sana in Corpore Sano)
- *"A Summerfieldian is confident in himself and proud of his achievements and has a real 'can do' attitude."*
- *"To find and develop those talents latent in each and every one of them."*
- *"Confidence gained outside the classroom will encourage boys to do well in the classroom."*
- *"It is often remarked that Summer Fields has a real family atmosphere."*

## 3. The Pilgrims' School Winchester
**thepilgrims-school.co.uk**

### Core Identity Statement

Pilgrims' has recently merged with Winchester College (effective September 2025), which has sharpened its messaging around **shared educational values** — specifically intellectual curiosity and independent thought. The school positions itself around **excellence in all its forms**, with a notable emphasis on oracy, character, and human connection alongside academic results.

The homepage headline:

> *"Welcome to a truly special prep school, centred around a quest for excellence in all its forms…"*

The merger announcement articulated the school's philosophy clearly:

> *"Winchester College and The Pilgrims' School have shared a common educational philosophy for nearly a century — one that values intellectual curiosity and independent thought."*

### How They Describe Teachers

Pilgrims' describes its teachers primarily through their pastoral impact and their relationship with boys — not their qualifications. Parent testimonials and inspection quotes do the heavy lifting:

> *"The genuine warmth shown by the teachers towards not just our son, but towards us as parents as well, is palpable and quite unique."*

> *"Pupils spontaneously say that their high levels of self-confidence and self-esteem are developed through the secure support provided by their teachers and pastoral leaders."* (ISI Inspection 2023)

> *"Boys are polite, supportive of one another and intellectually curious"* — Good Schools Guide, quoted on site

The headmaster Tim Butcher's own language focuses on **what teachers give boys the courage to do** rather than what teachers know or where they trained:

> *"These performances were about presence — about having the courage to stand up, find one's voice, and share something meaningful. Such moments are not merely academic exercises; they are rehearsals for life."*
> — Tim Butcher, Headmaster

### How They Frame Risk-Taking and Mistakes

Pilgrims' does not explicitly use "risk-taking" language, but frames development in terms of **courage and voice**:

> *"Think on their feet, respond to the unfamiliar, and bring a little of their authentic selves into the room."*

Forest School messaging uses more explicit developmental framing:

> *"Giving boys the time and space to develop at their own pace allows them to develop confidence and self-esteem. It enables them to achieve. Forest School empowers boys to take responsibility for their own learning by encouraging choice."*

The admissions policy framing is also telling — they look for boys who will:
> *"take a range of opportunities... bring a positive impact socially... make a rounded contribution to school life."*

### How They Frame Academic Achievement

Pilgrims' leads with breadth, not just depth. Academic results are contextualised within a holistic vision. The Good Schools Guide review is used prominently: *"There's something special here for almost every boy."*

The headmaster's framing is notably humanistic — academics are placed within a conversation about character:

> *"It is through such things — not just exams and outcomes, but voice, thoughtfulness, and human connection — that character is formed, and futures start to take shape."*
> — Tim Butcher, Headmaster

ISI inspection language is used selectively to validate specific claims:
> *"Achievement in music is exceptional... a fine example of excellence."*

Parent testimonials on academic outcomes are warm rather than clinical:
> *"Pilgrims' gives each boy a love of learning."*
> *"A school that maximises a boy's academic potential in a happy and nurturing community."*
> *"An absolute gem for boys to be themselves whilst flourishing and achieving beyond their potential."*

### Overall Tone

- **Personal, reflective, and community-focused** — the headmaster writes in a genuine first-person voice that is thoughtful rather than promotional.
- Strong emphasis on **oracy, presence, and personal expression** as educational goals in themselves.
- Warm but assured — not defensive or credential-heavy.
- Uses external validation (ISI, Good Schools Guide, Talk Education, Tatler) strategically to back up claims.
- The boarding community and cathedral setting give the messaging an organic sense of belonging.

### Standout Phrases

- *"A quest for excellence in all its forms"*
- *"Not just exams and outcomes, but voice, thoughtfulness, and human connection — that character is formed, and futures start to take shape."*
- *"Rehearsals for life"*
- *"Intellectual curiosity and independent thought"*
- *"An absolute gem for boys to be themselves whilst flourishing and achieving beyond their potential."*
- *"Boys are polite, supportive of one another and intellectually curious."*
- *"Bright, curious boys will thrive."*

## 4. Eton College
**etoncollege.com**

### Core Identity Statement

Eton's public messaging is deliberately measured — it carries the weight of its own reputation and does not need to oversell. The language is **assured, reflective, and purposeful**, written for an audience that already knows what Eton is. The Head Master, Simon Henderson, writes with strategic clarity rather than marketing warmth.

The published Purpose Statement (from the 2023 Strategy document, still the current framing on the website):

> *"The charity's primary purpose, determined from its Christian foundation in 1440, is to draw out the best of young people's talents and to enable them to flourish and make a positive impact on others through the course of a healthy, happy and fulfilling life."*

The six-point ethos commitment is unusually precise for a school website — it reads like a board-level governance document made public:

1. Encourages creativity, individuality, innovation and enjoyment through a broad vision of education
2. Enables boys to develop a strong understanding of themselves and their capabilities, while also empathising with others
3. Develops enquiring minds and fosters a life-long appreciation of independent thinking and learning
4. Develops physical and mental health, emotional maturity and spiritual richness
5. Nurtures the value of leadership and service
6. Celebrates kindness, loyalty, gratitude, integrity and determination

### How They Describe Teachers (Staff)

Eton's teacher description language is notably values-led and individuality-focused — again, no mention of qualifications or credentials:

> *"In serving the best interests of the boys it is essential to have a talented, motivated and happy body of staff who have a voice in defining a common vision and ethos and who feel genuinely valued for what they do."*

> *"Eton celebrates difference, not conformity, and members of staff are encouraged to express their individuality."*

The Recruitment Policy opens with this framing:
> *"Eton College is committed to promoting the best habits of independent thought and learning in the pursuit of excellence. We aim to enable everyone, pupils and staff, to discover their strengths and make the most of their talents within the College and beyond. There is no 'typical Etonian', likewise there is no typical member of Eton College staff."*

The devolved nature of the school is used as a structural statement about teachers:
> *"This deliberately gives greater autonomy to adults and to boys, who accept that with that autonomy comes responsibility and accountability."*

### How They Frame Risk-Taking and Mistakes

Eton does not use "risk-taking" language explicitly, but frames challenge and autonomy as inherent to the model:

> *"Boys are encouraged to pursue excellence in all its differing forms, to develop their strengths and to seek out new challenges."*

> *"The process being valued as much as the outcome."* — This is Eton's closest equivalent to "mistakes are welcome"; it signals that the journey matters, not just the grade.

> *"We recognise that there is no single route to success and are committed to offering a flexible, rigorous and enriching curriculum and co-curriculum where Etonians pursue excellence for its own sake."*

Simon Henderson's writing in Prospect Magazine (2022) captures the underlying philosophy:
> *"It's important that we don't forget to allow kids to have fun at school... That way, they'll be much more likely to challenge themselves to do the things they can't quite do yet."*

### How They Frame Academic Achievement

Eton almost never cites exam results or league tables in its public-facing messaging. Academic achievement is framed as **pursuing excellence for its own sake** — the outcome of a culture, not the goal of a curriculum.

> *"Etonians pursue excellence for its own sake, within and beyond the schoolroom."*

> *"We recognise that there is no single route to success."*

The language of scholarship and intellectual life is always contextualised within broader human development:

> *"An independent boys' boarding school... the primary purpose is to draw out the best of young people's talents and to enable them to flourish and make a positive impact on others."*

The Head Master's strategy letter ends with confident simplicity:
> *"Eton moves forward with ambition and with confidence. Floreat Etona!"*

### Overall Tone

- **Measured, confident, and institutional** — Eton writes like a charity trustee board with literary sensibility. It never needs to prove itself.
- The tone is **collaborative and consultative**: *"this plan is an invitation"*, *"shared endeavour"*, *"proactive interchange of ideas."*
- Tradition is acknowledged and honoured, but framed as dynamic rather than static: *"Eton is proud of its history and heritage... but the school has always had a dynamic relationship with its past."*
- The Head Master writes in the first person with quiet authority — never promotional, always purposeful.
- Uses the rhetoric of **stewardship** rather than achievement: this generation's role is to honour what came before while building what comes next.

### Standout Phrases

- *"Draw out the best of young people's talents"*
- *"A healthy, happy and fulfilling life"*
- *"The process being valued as much as the outcome"*
- *"Etonians pursue excellence for its own sake"*
- *"Enquiring minds and a life-long appreciation of independent thinking and learning"*
- *"Honouring tradition. Inspiring change."*
- *"There is no 'typical Etonian'"*
- *"Kindness, loyalty, gratitude, integrity and determination"*
- *"Shared endeavour"*
- *"Balancing the best of the old with the best of the new"*
- *"Floreat Etona!"*

## 5. The Independent School of Jakarta (Current Site)
**isj.id**

### Core Identity Statement

ISJ's current messaging is ambitious and well-written in places, but carries an **identity tension** throughout: it oscillates between leading with **credentials and systems** (UK-trained teachers, GL assessments, BSO framework) and the **character and joy** language that the best schools use naturally. The credential language often undercuts the warmth.

The homepage headline:

> *"A world class British education, for boys and girls, in the heart of Jakarta"*

The About page opens more promisingly:
> *"An ISJ education is an adventure. We support our pupils to become confident and creative learners, providing them with a safe and vibrant space to grow and thrive."*

### How They Currently Describe Teachers

This is where the tension is most visible. The homepage uses the phrase **"UK-Trained Teachers"** as a key data point in a four-column factbox — treating teacher origin as a credential rather than a character trait.

Meanwhile, the Welcome Letter uses much warmer language:

> *"Our exceptionally skilled and experienced staff provide them with deeply personalised support and encouragement."*

And the Why ISJ page includes a staff quote that demonstrates the character-led approach perfectly:

> *"Children who feel valued are far more likely to try new things and push themselves. They see their teacher as a partner in their learning journey, not just an authority figure."*
> — Lesley Brooks, Senior Teacher

> *"A child's language is part of their identity. When we honour it, we honour the whole child."*
> — Erik Boswinkel, Early Years Teacher

The Why ISJ page does describe teacher recruitment in more aspirational terms:
> *"ISJ recruits teachers from respected UK independent schools and well-established international schools, bringing with them strong subject knowledge, professional confidence and a clear understanding of British curriculum expectations."*

But the factbox labels — "UK-Trained Teachers" as a bullet point — sits at odds with the emotional and character-led framing that the best comparable schools use.

### How They Frame Risk-Taking and Mistakes

ISJ uses risk-taking language prominently and consistently — this is one of the site's genuine strengths:

> *"Children are encouraged to take risks and to question rather than just accept."* (Homepage)

> *"Children are encouraged to take risks, to step out of their comfort zone, to discover new things and experiences and in the process to gain confidence and develop character."* (Homepage)

> *"Pupils are encouraged to take risks, develop critical thinking, and embrace the joy of discovery."* (About page)

This language is well-placed. It mirrors Dragon and Pilgrims' ethos and is handled naturally.

### How They Frame Academic Achievement

ISJ frames academic results through **benchmarked data** (GL Education scores) — a transparency that is distinctive and commercially useful for the internationally mobile market. But this sometimes crowds out the character-led framing:

> *"Our pupils consistently achieve results in the top 10% in English, Mathematics, and Science — placing ISJ among the leading British international schools globally."*

The Why ISJ page gives specific GL Education Standard Age Scores (English: 122 vs 104.6 international mean; Maths: 118.7 vs 101; Science: 119.7 vs 103.1) — these are compelling evidence but are delivered in a way that reads more like an investor deck than a school's character statement.

The best version of ISJ's academic framing is in the Welcome Letter:

> *"They learn how to think, not what to think."* — This is excellent and deserves to be a centrepiece phrase.

> *"Exemplary and dynamic teaching that preserves their joy in learning while also developing the discipline of study as they mature."*

### Overall Tone

- **Mixed** — alternates between warm/personal and clinical/institutional within the same pages.
- The Welcome Letter (Eileen Fisher) is the strongest piece of writing on the site: genuinely warm, parent-facing, and emotionally resonant.
- The Why ISJ page reads more like a prospectus document than a story — comprehensive, but not compelling.
- The Homepage oscillates: the opening philosophy paragraph is good, but the four-column factbox ("UK-Trained Teachers / English National Curriculum / Part of The Schools Trust / Central Pondok Indah") immediately shifts into institutional bullet-point mode.
- There is no consistent **identity noun** for ISJ pupils the way Dragon has "Dragons" and Summer Fields has "Summerfieldians." This is a gap.

### What Currently Works Well

- The risk-taking language is genuine and well-used
- *"They learn how to think, not what to think"* is the strongest single phrase on the site
- The Welcome Letter tone from Eileen Fisher is warm, personal, and well-written
- The staff quotes (Lesley Brooks, Erik Boswinkel, Grace Wang) are distinctive and character-rich
- The community framing ("children are known by name, their unique talents are celebrated, and their voices are heard") is strong
- *"Ambition meets empathy"* — an excellent pairing worth using more prominently
- *"A joyful and fulfilled childhood"* — resonant parental language

### What Creates Friction

- "UK-Trained Teachers" as a credential label rather than a character quality
- Overuse of "benchmarked" and "standardised" — useful for some audiences, cold for most
- The four-column facts box on the homepage front-loads systems over story
- "BSO framework" and "ISI or Penta International" in the About page — important for credibility but misplaced in narrative sections
- No consistent school identity name for pupils

## Key Takeaways for ISJ v4

1. **The best schools never lead with credentials** — they lead with character, identity, and philosophy. Credentials appear later, as evidence, not as the opening pitch.

2. **"UK-Trained Teachers" is a credential label, not a character statement.** Compare Dragon's *"passionate subject specialists"* or Summer Fields' *"a passion to teach the boys of tomorrow."* ISJ already has great staff quotes on the site — those should be elevated above the factbox.

3. **ISJ's risk-taking language is genuinely strong** — it matches Dragon and Pilgrims' in spirit. This should be amplified, not buried.

4. **The single strongest phrase currently on the ISJ site** is: *"They learn how to think, not what to think."* This deserves prominent placement — it belongs on the homepage, in the head's welcome, and in the brand identity. Every comparable school uses a version of this idea.

5. **ISJ needs a pupil identity name.** Dragon has "Dragons." Summer Fields has "Summerfieldians." This creates belonging and pride. ISJ could explore equivalents.

6. **The Welcome Letter is the best piece of writing on the site** — it should set the tone for the entire homepage, not compete with a facts grid beneath it.

7. **"Ambition meets empathy"** — this phrase from the About page is punchy and distinctive. It currently sits buried. It could work as a key descriptor.

8. **Eton's "process valued as much as outcome"** is the most elegant framing of the mistakes/risk philosophy. ISJ's version — "take risks, step out of their comfort zone" — is good but could be sharpened with a phrase that frames the culture rather than just the instruction.
