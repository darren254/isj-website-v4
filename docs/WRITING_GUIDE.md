# ISJ Writing Guide — Insights & News Articles

**Read alongside `ARTICLE_STANDARDS.md`.**
This guide covers the writing craft. ARTICLE_STANDARDS.md covers technical requirements (props, SEO rules, quality checklist). They do not overlap — both apply.

---

## Who You Are Writing For

The primary reader is an expat parent or internationally-mobile professional, currently researching Jakarta, international schools, or both. They are educated, time-poor, and sceptical of marketing language. They have read a lot of school websites and can smell copy that was written to impress rather than to inform.

Secondary reader: Indonesian parents considering an international school. Treat them identically. Do not dumb down, do not over-explain.

Write for the specific, not the average. A reader deciding between neighbourhoods wants granular detail, not reassurance that Jakarta is "a vibrant city with lots to offer."

---

## Voice

One register throughout: knowledgeable local friend. Not a school brochure. Not a lifestyle magazine. Not a Wikipedia entry.

That means:
- Direct over diplomatic
- Specific over general
- Earned confidence, not performed enthusiasm
- No adjective-stacking ("fantastic, world-class, inspiring")
- No filler openers ("Jakarta is a city like no other...")

The test: would a well-informed person in Jakarta say this sentence in conversation? If not, rewrite it.

---

## The TL;DR (`summary` prop)

This renders automatically as the first thing a reader sees after the headline. It is not a teaser or a hook — it is a factual summary of what the article concludes.

**Write it last.** After the article is done, distil it into 1–3 sentences.

**Criteria:**
- Specific and honest. If the article concludes that Pondok Indah suits families but Kemang suits younger professionals, say that.
- No teasing ("Read on to find out..."). The reader wants the answer, not a promise.
- No superlatives. "The most important thing" is a cliché. State the thing.
- Does not have to cover every section. Pick the one or two things that matter most.

**Good:**
> Jakarta is a large and complicated city. Where families end up living makes an enormous difference to daily life. This guide covers the areas that expat and internationally-minded families actually choose, and what drives those decisions.

**Bad:**
> Discover everything you need to know about living in Jakarta as an expat family. Read on for our expert guide.

## Opening Paragraph

The opening paragraph must earn the reader's attention without performing for it. No throat-clearing. No scene-setting that doesn't add information.

**Rules:**
- First sentence establishes the real-world stakes or the concrete situation.
- Primary keyword appears naturally in the first paragraph — not shoehorned into the first sentence.
- Do not open with a rhetorical question.
- Do not open with a statistic unless it is surprising and directly relevant.
- Do not open with "If you're an expat family thinking about..."

**Good opening (from best-neighbourhoods-jakarta.astro):**
> Jakarta is a city of 30 million people across a metropolitan area the size of greater London. Choosing a neighbourhood is one of the most consequential decisions a relocating family makes. Get it right and daily life is manageable. Get it wrong and school runs become two-hour ordeals, weekends feel unreachable, and the city grinds you down.

**What makes it work:** Immediate concrete stakes. No marketing tone. The scale comparison (greater London) is useful information, not decoration.

---

## H2 Section Headings

H2 headings are navigational anchors and SEO signals. They should be specific enough that a reader skimming the page understands the article structure without reading the body.

**Rules:**
- Descriptive, not clever. "Pondok Indah" is better than "The Green Heart of South Jakarta."
- Primary keyword or close variant in at least one H2.
- 2–4 words for neighbourhood/place articles. Full question format acceptable for education articles ("What Does the English National Curriculum Cover?").
- No H3s unless a section is genuinely complex enough to need sub-sections. Most articles do not need them.

---

## Body Paragraphs

**Length:** 3–5 sentences. No one-liners. No walls of text.

**Pacing:** Each paragraph makes one point. The next paragraph develops or contrasts it. Avoid restating the previous paragraph in slightly different words.

**Specificity:** One vivid, verifiable detail beats three general statements.

- Bad: "Pondok Indah is a popular choice for families because of its amenities and pleasant atmosphere."
- Good: "Pondok Indah Mall is a full-service lifestyle destination. There is a golf course, a water park, an international hospital, and enough cafes, restaurants, and supermarkets that families can meet most daily needs without going far."

**Trade-offs:** Good Jakarta Life and Education articles acknowledge what something costs, what the downside is, what the caveat is. Readers trust copy that tells them the catch. Copy that only presents the positive reads as marketing.

## SEO — Writing It In, Not Bolting It On

SEO rules from `ARTICLE_STANDARDS.md` (keyword in title, first paragraph, one H2; meta description 70–160 chars) are non-negotiable. This section covers how to do it without the writing feeling forced.

**Primary keyword placement:**
- Title: include it naturally. If the title has to sound awkward to include the keyword, rewrite the title.
- First paragraph: use the keyword in a sentence that would have been written that way regardless. Do not open with the keyword.
- H2: use the keyword or a close semantic variant in one section heading. Usually the section where you most directly address the query intent.

**Secondary keywords:**
- Use naturally throughout. Do not assign a secondary keyword to each paragraph.
- Synonyms and related terms (e.g. "South Jakarta," "expat neighbourhood," "international school catchment") help without keyword-stuffing.

**Meta description:**
- Write it as a sentence a human would read, not a keyword list.
- State what the article covers and the key conclusion or value.
- 70–160 characters — check with a character counter before committing.
- Example: "Where expat families actually live in Jakarta. A practical guide to South Jakarta, the SCBD corridor, Bintaro, and the areas that matter when you have children." (162 chars — trim to fit.)

**Internal links:**
- One natural internal link near the end of the article, to a related ISJ page. It should read as a useful next step, not a navigation prompt.
- Good: "Families weighing the South Jakarta corridor often start with the commute — the [ISJ commute times tool](/tools/commute-times) maps journey times from the main expat neighbourhoods."
- Bad: "Click here to learn more about ISJ."

## ISJ References in Jakarta Life Articles

Jakarta Life articles are not about ISJ. ISJ gets one mention per article, natural and contextual. It should be the mention that would appear if a knowledgeable local were describing the area — not a forced plug.

- Correct context: naming ISJ as one of several schools in a neighbourhood, when schools are the relevant topic.
- Incorrect context: ending every section with a reference to ISJ's curriculum or values.

See `ARTICLE_STANDARDS.md` Section 8 for additional Jakarta Life rules.

---

## Education Articles

Education articles (curriculum, pedagogy, school selection) can reference ISJ more directly since the article is about a topic ISJ has relevant expertise in. The tone shifts slightly — more authoritative, less local-friend. Still not a brochure.

**Rules:**
- Do not name exam boards (Edexcel, AQA, Cambridge, OCR). Refer to "GCSEs" and "A-Levels" without specifying the board.
- Do not state fees. Link to the fees page if fee context is needed.
- Do not negatively evaluate competitor schools. Naming them as factual reference points is fine.
- Back claims with specifics. "ISJ uses a through-school model from Reception to Sixth Form" is better than "ISJ provides a complete educational journey."

## Length

| Article type | Target length |
|---|---|
| Jakarta Life (neighbourhood, area guide) | 700–1,100 words |
| Jakarta Life (practical: healthcare, cost of living) | 800–1,200 words |
| Education (curriculum, pedagogy) | 600–900 words |
| News article | 300–500 words |

Long is not better. If the article is finished at 700 words, publish at 700. Do not pad to hit a target.

## What Not to Write

These are the patterns most likely to appear in AI-assisted drafts. Eliminate them on review.

| Pattern | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Adjective stacking | "a vibrant, dynamic, world-class city" | Pick one or drop all |
| Empty opener | "Jakarta is a city like no other" | State a fact instead |
| Filler transition | "It is also worth noting that..." | Delete and start the point |
| Marketing superlative | "ISJ's exceptional, award-winning faculty" | State the specific thing |
| Teaser summary | "Read on to discover everything you need to know" | State the answer |
| False balance | "While some families prefer X, others prefer Y" (with no further information) | Say which and why |
| Hedged claim | "Jakarta can sometimes be a challenging city to navigate" | Be direct: "Jakarta traffic is the defining constraint of daily life" |
| We/our for ISJ | "We offer a broad curriculum" | "ISJ offers a broad curriculum" |

## Final Check — Writing Quality

Run this before committing, in addition to the `ARTICLE_STANDARDS.md` quality checklist:

- [ ] TL;DR (`summary` prop) written after the article, not before. Specific and honest.
- [ ] Opening paragraph has no throat-clearing. Real stakes in the first two sentences.
- [ ] Every H2 is descriptive enough to navigate by.
- [ ] Each paragraph makes one point.
- [ ] At least one verifiable specific detail per section.
- [ ] Trade-offs or caveats present where they exist in reality.
- [ ] Primary keyword placed naturally (not forced) in title, first paragraph, one H2.
- [ ] Meta description reads as a sentence, 70–160 characters.
- [ ] No adjective stacking, empty openers, filler transitions.
- [ ] Internal link reads as a useful next step, not a navigation prompt.
- [ ] Article ends when the subject is covered — not padded to a word count.
